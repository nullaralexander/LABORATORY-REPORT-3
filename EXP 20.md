# EXPERIMENT 20: Undersampling in SDR (Software Defined Radio)  
---

# I. INTRODUCTION
Software Defined Radio (SDR) represents a transformative shift in electronic communications, offering a flexible, software-based alternative to traditional hardware receivers that are often restricted to specific frequency bands or modulation schemes. By converting radio signals to digital data and utilizing software for decoding, a single SDR tuner can process various formats—such as AM, FM, ASK, and DSSS—and can be easily updated to accommodate future technologies. A key challenge for SDR is processing high-frequency signals, such as 2.4GHz cellular transmissions, which would normally require extremely high sampling rates under the standard Nyquist Criterion. However, since radio transmissions are bandwidth-limited signals without components near DC, they can be processed using a technique known as undersampling (or band-pass sampling). According to Shannon’s Information Theorem, all information in such a signal can be captured at a rate as low as twice the signal’s bandwidth rather than twice the carrier frequency. Remarkably, when the undersampling frequency is correctly matched to twice the bandwidth, the process inherently demodulates the signal, allowing the original message to be recovered simply by using a low-pass filter to isolate the message from higher-frequency aliases. This experiment utilizes the Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 to implement a bandwidth-limited signal, demonstrate the use of undersampling for demodulation, and investigate the effects of frequency mismatches on the recovered message.

# 1. PART A – Setting up a bandwidth limited signal
To begin the exploration of undersampling, a bandwidth-limited signal must first be established. For this experiment, a DSBSC signal is utilized due to its simplicity in wiring, generated by modulating a 100kHz carrier with a 2kHz sine wave message. After setting the oscilloscope trigger to CH1 and viewing approximately two cycles of the 2kHz SINE output, the scope is switched to DUAL mode to allow for a simultaneous comparison of the message and the modulated output. By adjusting the vertical attenuation to 1V/div for Channel 1 and 2V/div for Channel 2, the Multiplier module's output can be clearly observed. This output is a characteristic DSBSC signal, where alternating halves of the envelope mirror the shape of the original sine wave message, providing a well-defined bandwidth-limited source for the subsequent undersampling stages.

## 1.1 Experiment Circuit Procedure
The system is configured to produce a 100kHz carrier that undergoes DSBSC modulation using a 2kHz sine wave as the message signal. This process results in a bandwidth-limited transmission where the carrier is suppressed, leaving the message information contained within the sidebands.

# 2. PART B – Direct down-conversion using undersampling
The experiment transitions to direct down-conversion by leveraging the mathematical property that a sampled signal is equivalent to the multiplication of the sampling signal and the message. When a bandwidth-limited signal like DSBSC is sampled, the output contains duplicates of the sidebands and multiple aliases at frequencies corresponding to the sum and difference of the message and the sampling signal's fundamental and harmonic components. While standard Nyquist theory suggests a sampling rate of at least 204kHz for a 100kHz carrier to avoid aliasing, the bandwidth-limited nature of the signal allows for much lower undersampling rates as long as the aliases do not overlap the desired signal components. Crucially, by selecting a sampling rate that is a whole integer sub-multiple of the carrier frequency—and at least twice the signal's bandwidth—the sampling action functions as a product detector. This process effectively "multiplies" the modulated carrier by the sampling signal's harmonics, causing one of the aliases to center at DC (baseband). This allows the original 2kHz message to be directly recovered using only a low-pass filter, bypassing the need for complex intermediate frequency stages. 

## 2.1 Experiment Circuit Procedure
In this setup, the Multiplier module is responsible for generating the bandwidth-limited DSBSC modulated carrier. To recover the original message, the Sample-and-Hold circuit acts as an undersampler, effectively down-converting the high-frequency signal, while the Baseband LPF isolates the resulting baseband alias to complete the demodulation process.

# 3. PART C- Synchronisation
The experiment explores the critical role of synchronization in SDR systems, emphasizing that direct down-conversion via undersampling is a form of product detection. Just as in standard product detection, any frequency or phase error between the local sampling signal and the modulated carrier results in signal degradation. Phase errors can lead to magnitude reduction or complete signal cancellation, while frequency errors cause effects ranging from periodic inaudibility to a total loss of intelligibility depending on the severity of the mismatch. To demonstrate these effects, the VCO module is configured to the LO range and adjusted to produce a digital output of approximately 8.333kHz (a 120µs period). By substituting this independent VCO signal for the synchronized 8.333kHz clock previously used, the setup simulates a real-world scenario where the receiver's local oscillator is not perfectly locked to the transmitter. Because it is highly unlikely for the VCO to match the required frequency and phase exactly, the resulting instability in the recovered message provides a practical look at the synchronization challenges inherent in software-defined radio architectures.

## 3.1 Experiment Circuit Procedure
In this modification, the synchronized 8.333kHz clock (labeled as the 8kHz DIGITAL output) from the Master Signals module is replaced with an independent 8.333kHz digital signal generated by the VCO module. This change highlights the necessity of synchronization, as it is highly improbable that the free-running VCO will maintain the precise frequency and phase relationship required for stable demodulation. Without this exact lock, the undersampling process fails to perfectly down-convert the carrier, leading to visible instability or errors in the recovered message.

# V. Lab Assessment: Post-Experiment Questions

**Question 1: Question 1: For the given inputs to the Multiplier module, what are the frequencies of the two sinewaves that make up the DSBSC signal?**

Answer: The DSBSC signal is composed of two sidebands: the Lower Sideband (LSB) and the Upper Sideband (USB). These frequencies are calculated by subtracting and adding the message frequency (2kHz) from the carrier frequency (100kHz):
*	LSB: 100kHz – 2kHz= 98kHZ
*	USB: 100kHz + 2kHz = 102kHz

**Question 2: What's the bandwidth of the DSBSC signal?**

Answer: The bandwidth of a DSBSC signal is twice the frequency of the modulating message. With a 2kHz sine wave message, the bandwidth is 4kHz (extending from 98kHz to 102kHz).

**Question 3: What's the significance of the signal on the Baseband LPF's output?**

Answer: The signal at the output of the Baseband LPF represents the successfully demodulated message. Its significance lies in the fact that it was recovered using undersampling at a rate significantly lower than the carrier frequency, demonstrating that SDR can down-convert high-frequency signals directly to baseband without intermediate hardware stages.

**Question 4: Given the sampling frequency is 8.333kHz (the signal's specified value of 8kHz is rounded down for simplicity), which harmonic in the sampling signal is demodulating the DSBSC signal?**

Answer: Undersampling functions as a product detector when a harmonic of the sampling frequency matches the carrier frequency. To find the specific harmonic (n):
n = Carrier Frequency/Sampling Frequency = 100,000Hz/8,333.33Hz = 12
The 12th harmonic of the sampling signal is the component responsible for demodulating the DSBSC signal.


**Question 5: Why doesn't this solve the problem and allow the demodulator to recover the message?**

Answer: (Referring to the use of a non-synchronized VCO) Simply matching the frequency (e.g., setting the VCO to 8.333Hz) does not solve the problem because phase and frequency synchronization are both essential. Because the VCO is an independent "free-running" source, it is highly unlikely to match the transmitter's carrier phase or maintain the exact frequency sub-multiple over time. This lack of synchronization causes the recovered message to fluctuate in amplitude or phase, resulting in poor fidelity or complete signal loss.

# VI. Conclusion
Experiment 20 successfully demonstrated the operational principles and advantages of Software Defined Radio (SDR) through the use of undersampling (band-pass sampling) for direct down-conversion. By utilizing the Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101, the following key insights were validated:
*	Efficiency of Undersampling: The experiment proved that a high-frequency 100kHz DSBSC carrier can be successfully demodulated using a sampling rate as low as 8.333kHz, significantly lower than the standard Nyquist requirement of 204kHz. This confirms that for bandwidth-limited signals, capturing the information requires a rate only twice the signal's bandwidth, not twice its highest frequency.
*	Simplified Architecture: By utilizing a Sample-and-Hold circuit and a Baseband LPF, the system performed direct down-conversion to baseband. This process leverages sampling harmonics to act as a product detector, bypassing the need for traditional, complex intermediate frequency (IF) hardware stages.
*	Criticality of Synchronization: The substitution of a synchronized clock with a free-running VCO highlighted that SDR performance is highly dependent on precise frequency and phase locking. Even minor mismatches resulted in visible instability, "beating," and distortion in the recovered 2kHz sine wave, illustrating the synchronization challenges inherent in SDR architectures.
*	Recovered Signal Integrity: Observations confirmed that while the raw sampled output contains high-frequency aliases and "stepping" noise, a standard Low-pass Filter is sufficient to isolate and reconstruct a recognizable version of the original message.
In summary, the experiment validated the core principle of SDR: that sophisticated, flexible communication receivers can be implemented by combining high-speed sampling with software-defined parameters, provided that rigorous synchronization is maintained between the transmitter and receiver.
