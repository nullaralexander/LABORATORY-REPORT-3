# EXPERIMENT 14: Bandwidth Limiting and Restoring Digital Signals

---

# I. INTRODUCTION
In a classical communications model, information travels from a transmitter to a receiver through a channel, such as copper wire, optical fiber, or free space. Every transmission medium possesses a specific bandwidth, meaning it functions as a filter that allows a certain range of frequencies to pass while attenuating or eliminating others. This frequency restriction has significant consequences for signal integrity.
Analog and digital signals are composed of multiple sine waves, including fundamentals and harmonics. If a channel's bandwidth is insufficient, these essential components are lost, leading to signal distortion. For digital communications, such as Pulse Code Modulation (PCM), severe distortion can make it nearly impossible for a receiver to correctly interpret logic levels. When a decoder misinterprets these bits, it generates incorrect voltages, resulting in a "noisy" or corrupted recovered message.
In this experiment, you will utilize the Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 to construct a PCM communications system and model a bandwidth-limited channel using a low-pass filter. You will evaluate the impact of this limitation by observing the PCM data on an oscilloscope and listening to the resulting degradation of the recovered audio. Finally, you will implement a comparator to attempt to restore the distorted digital signal and analyze the limitations of this restoration method.

# 2. PART B – Decoding the PCM data
The experiment examines how restricted channel bandwidth physically alters the shape of digital pulses by using a stable data stream from the Sequence Generator module. After dismantling the previous setup, the scope is configured to EXT trigger mode using the generator’s SYNC output to ensure a stable display of the 32-bit sequence. Initially, with the Tuneable Low-pass Filter’s cut-off frequency set fully clockwise, the bandwidth-limited signal on Channel 2 closely resembles the sharp, rectangular pulses of the original digital signal on Channel 1. However, as the cut-off frequency is rotated counter-clockwise, the effects of bandwidth limiting become visible: the sharp edges of the pulses begin to round off, and the signal amplitude decreases. This occurs because the filter attenuates the high-frequency harmonics necessary to maintain a square waveform, eventually distorting the signal to a point where individual bits become difficult to distinguish.

This level of distortion makes it nearly impossible for a digital receiver to identify the original logic levels, as the signal no longer reaches clear "high" or "low" voltage thresholds, effectively rendering the digital data unreadable.


## 2.2 Experiment Circuit Procedure
One solution to the issues caused by channel bandwidth limiting is to utilize transmission media with a sufficiently wide bandwidth for digital data. While certain cable designs offer better frequency responses, the increasing demand for high-speed data often requires pushing more information through existing channels. To achieve higher data rates without slowing the system down, the transmission bit rate must be increased, which effectively creates the same distortion as reducing the channel's bandwidth.
To model this effect, begin by rotating the Tuneable Low-pass Filter’s Cut-off Frequency control fully clockwise to provide the widest possible bandwidth. Set the VCO module to the LO range and adjust its Frequency control to the "9 o'clock" position, ensuring the oscilloscope display is stable. To simulate a higher bit rate, gradually turn the VCO Frequency control clockwise while simultaneously adjusting the scope's Timebase to maintain a clear view of the digital signals.

## 2.3 EYE DIAGRAM
Bandwidth limiting significantly distorts digital signals, potentially leading to receiver misinterpretation, which makes it essential to inspect and test channel performance. Eye diagrams are an ideal tool for this assessment and are generated by changing the oscilloscope's trigger source. To begin, reduce the bandwidth limiting by returning the VCO Frequency control to its midpoint and adjusting the scope’s timebase to 20μs/div.
By triggering the scope with the system clock rather than the SYNC signal, the scope overlays all data bits on top of each other, creating the "eye" pattern. When the digital signal is relatively unaffected by the channel, the spaces between the traces—the "eyes"—appear wide open. However, increasing the bit rate by turning the VCO clockwise or restricting the bandwidth by turning the Low-pass Filter counter-clockwise causes these eyes to close. After observing these effects, reset the system by returning the VCO to its midpoint, setting the filter to maximum bandwidth, and reconnecting the scope to the Sequence Generator’s SYNC output.

# 3. PART C- Encoding and decoding speech
Bandwidth limiting inevitably distorts digital signals, and these effects worsen as the transmission bit rate increases, causing significant issues for receivers like PCM decoders. To address this, the received signal must be "restored" or cleaned up using a comparator before the decoding process. The comparator functions by amplifying the difference between its two inputs, effectively "squaring-up" a bandwidth-limited signal when it swings above and below a reference DC voltage. By connecting the distorted signal to one input and a variable DC voltage to the other, the comparator produces a clipped output that can be adjusted to replicate the original digital signal.
However, this restoration method has specific limitations, as extreme bandwidth distortion can prevent the comparator from accurately recovering the data without errors. Additionally, the channel may introduce a phase shift in the received signal, which can lead to further complications for the receiver. To begin this part of the experiment, set the oscilloscope's timebase to 0.5ms/div and position the Variable DCV control at the midpoint of its travel. Finally, disconnect the patch lead from the VCO module's DIGITAL output to prepare for the modified circuit configuration.

# V. Lab Assessment: Post-Experiment Questions

**Question 1:Why does bandwidth limiting of the channel cause the PCM Decoder module to output incorrect voltages as well as the correct one?**

Answer: Bandwidth limiting removes the high-frequency harmonics required to maintain sharp "edges" on the digital pulses. When these pulses become rounded and attenuated, the decoder can no longer reliably distinguish between logic high and logic low levels, leading it to misinterpret the 8-bit digital codes and generate incorrect analog voltages.

**Question 2: If this were a communications system transmitting speech, what would these errors sound like when the message is reconstructed?**

Answer: In a PCM system, these bit errors manifest as audible noise, typically heard as "clicks" or "crackling" sounds in the reconstructed speech. As the distortion increases and more bits are misinterpreted, the speech would eventually become completely unintelligible.

**Question 3: What two things are happening to cause the digital signal to change shape?**

Answer: The change in shape is caused by attenuation (a reduction in signal amplitude) and the loss of high-frequency harmonics. Because a square wave is composed of a fundamental frequency and many harmonics, removing the higher frequencies rounds off the sharp corners of the pulses.

**Question 4:What other change to your communication system distorts the digital signal in the same way as increasing its bit-rate?**

Answer: Decreasing the channel's bandwidth (by turning the low-pass filter's cut-off frequency down) distorts the signal in the exact same way as increasing the bit-rate. Both actions reduce the ratio of available bandwidth to the frequency of the data pulses.

**Question 5: Although the restored digital signal is almost identical to the original digital signal, there is a difference. Can you see what it is?**

Answer: The difference is a phase shift or time delay. When overlaying the signals, the restored yellow trace is shifted horizontally to the right compared to the original red trace.

**Question 6: Can this difference be ignored? Why?**

Answer: In many simple systems, this slight delay can be ignored because the logical sequence of the bits remains intact. however, in high-speed or synchronous systems, excessive phase shift can cause timing issues where the receiver samples the data at the wrong moment, leading to errors.

**Question 7: Why do some DC voltages cause the comparator to output the wrong information?**

Answer: If the DC reference voltage is set too high or too low, the distorted (rounded) signal may not cross the threshold at the correct times, or may not cross it at all. This results in the comparator "missing" bits or outputting pulses with the wrong width (duty cycle).

**Question 8: Why does the comparator begin to output the wrong information when this control (bandwidth/frequency) is turned far enough?**

Answer: When bandwidth is restricted or bit-rate is increased too much, the signal amplitude drops so low that it no longer crosses the comparator's DC reference threshold. At this point, the comparator can no longer "see" the transitions between 1s and 0s.

**Question 9: How can the comparator restore the bandwidth limited digital signal when it is so distorted?**

Answer: The comparator acts as a high-gain switching device that triggers a clean, full-swing output the moment the input signal crosses a specific DC threshold. By "clipping" the rounded signal at this transition point, it regenerates the sharp vertical edges of the original square wave.

# VI. Conclusion
This experiment successfully demonstrated the critical relationship between channel bandwidth, transmission bit rate, and digital signal integrity. Initially, the PCM system accurately reconstructed analog voltages when the channel bandwidth was sufficient to pass necessary high-frequency harmonics. However, restricting the bandwidth through low-pass filtering removed these harmonics, causing rectangular pulses to become rounded and attenuated, eventually leading to severe corruption of the decoded output as logic levels became indistinguishable. This same distortion was observed when increasing the transmission bit rate, confirming that high-speed data requires a proportionally wider bandwidth to prevent intersymbol interference.
The use of eye diagrams provided a vital diagnostic tool, visually illustrating signal quality through the "openness" of the eye. Wide-open eyes indicated clear logic states, while "closing" eyes signaled increased timing jitter and rounding that lead to bit errors. While the introduction of a comparator proved effective for "squaring up" distorted signals by triggering at a specific DC reference threshold, this restoration method could not eliminate the inherent phase shift introduced by the channel. Ultimately, the experiment highlights that while hardware solutions like comparators can recover pulse shapes, there are fundamental physical limits to data transmission: if distortion is extreme enough to prevent the signal from crossing the switching threshold, the digital information is permanently lost.
